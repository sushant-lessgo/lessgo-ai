# Work sites, end to end — from one line to a live site that converts

**Who this is for:** people whose work sells them. Photographers, designers, writers,
agencies, architects, artists. A visitor looks at their work, believes, and reaches out.

**Scope (agreed):** this flow is designed ONCE for ALL work sellers — not per profession.
The steps, the 8 fact slots, the grouping machinery, the promises: identical for a
photographer, a designer, an agency. What changes per profession is only the surface:
question wording, the choice-chips offered, the fallback labels ("galleries" vs
"projects" vs "case studies"), and which media the work arrives as. One honest v1 limit:
work ingestion starts image-shaped (photographers, designers, visual folk); text-work
sellers (writers) and video-work sellers plug into the same slots later with their own
media handling.

**The only instruction:** we are building a HIGH-CONVERTING website builder in the true
sense. Every step below exists to serve one sentence:

> A visitor lands, sees the work, believes it, and takes one action.

Everything that doesn't serve that sentence gets cut.

---

## What makes a work site actually convert (the visitor's side)

Before the journey — this is the logic every step must serve:

1. **The work is the argument.** Not the headline, not the copy. The best work, first,
   big, fast-loading. Copy only frames it.
2. **Proof lives next to the work.** Real client names, real praise, real results —
   beside the project they belong to, not in a quote graveyard at the bottom.
3. **Prices pre-qualify.** A clear price signal (even a range) filters dreamers from
   buyers before they message. Fewer, better leads.
4. **One action per page.** Every page ends at exactly one thing to do. A site can have
   several goals — each page carries one.
5. **Phone first.** Their visitors come from an Instagram tap. If it isn't instant and
   beautiful on a phone, nothing else matters.

## The journey: one line → live site

**Journey-wide rule (agreed): the "What we understood" rail.** From the moment we know
anything, a side panel shows our live understanding of them — name, what they do, where,
price position, languages, what they sell — updating as ingestion and answers fill it in.
It stays on screen THROUGHOUT the journey, every step. Correcting it is a tap, any time.
This kills confirmation-as-a-step forever: we never replay answers back as a blocking
screen, we just always show what we believe and keep it correctable. The "something
wrong? tell us" free-text box lives at the bottom of this rail.

### 1. One line in
"I photograph weddings in Amsterdam." That's the whole start.

From this one line we work out ourselves: who hires them, what those buyers need to
see to believe, and what action fits (WhatsApp, booking, form). We never ask the user
anything we can figure out.

**Value:** zero-effort start. Momentum from second one.

### 2. Show us your work — the one thing only they have
Point us at it: Instagram, existing website, a folder of images, Google Drive. We pull
it in and do the curation work for them: pick the strongest pieces, lead with the best,
group them into projects, pull real client praise (reviews, testimonials, names) along
the way — used word for word, never rewritten.

A work site without their work is a costume. This step is the heart of the whole
product, not an upload field buried in a wizard.

**Agreed (brainstorm #1):**
- **v1 sources = existing website + plain upload (drag a folder).** The existing site also
  carries their own curation — what they put on their homepage is their pick of best work.
  Instagram later, best-effort only (paste URL, we try, graceful fallback) — never
  load-bearing. Google reviews: not in v1.
- **Picking "the best" is hybrid, their taste first:** (1) their own curation signals
  (homepage/hero placement, album structure) outrank everything; (2) a no-taste technical
  filter kills blurry/tiny/screenshots/near-duplicate frames; (3) AI taste only ranks
  what's left, to fill gaps — it never overrules their choices. Curation is always a
  proposal the user can correct by tap, never a silent verdict.
- Cap ingestion (~150 in → ~30 the site needs). Every image goes through the speed
  pipeline (resize, modern format, blur placeholder) — fast-on-phone is non-negotiable.
- Pilot fixture: Kundius's real photos.
- **Grouping: work is grouped by what a buyer shops for = what the person sells.**
  Work groups ≙ services ≙ price list — one spine: visitor wants weddings → sees wedding
  work → sees wedding package → messages. Never a free-form filing exercise.
- **Groups propose themselves** (never homework), signals in order of trust: their
  existing site's albums/pages → upload folder names ("folders become groups") → photo
  timestamps (same-day cluster = one shoot) → AI look-at-the-image as last resort.
  Correction = one screen of visual cards, five verbs, all taps: rename, merge, drag
  between, hide, pick cover.
- Two seller kinds, same machinery: **category sellers** (photographer: groups = service
  categories, browsed as galleries — v1 centers here) and **story sellers** (agency/
  designer: group = case study with client + problem + result; v1 carries the fields,
  full case-study treatment later).
- User-facing wording: never internal terms; groups wear the user's own names; fallback
  label is profession-appropriate ("galleries" for photographers, "projects" for
  designers) via the question-wording personalization layer.

**Value:** the site IS their portfolio. Also the biggest "how did it do that" moment.

### 3. A few questions only they can answer

**Agreed (brainstorm #2) — the full fact list is 8 slots; most auto-fill, so a person
sees ~2-6 actual questions:**

1. Name/business + where + how far you go *(usually harvested from the one line / their site)*
2. What you sell = the work groups *(from ingestion, confirmed on the grouping screen)*
3. **Price per service — exact / from-X / on request** *(usually asked; REQUIRED step —
   prices pre-qualify buyers, it's a conversion pillar. "On request" is a legal answer —
   premium sellers need it)*
4. New or established? *(a BRANCH, not just a fact: established → proof-led story;
   new → fresh-eyes story, no fake proof ever)*
5. Who's the dream client? *(chips, pre-narrowed by what the work shows)*
6. What do clients praise you most? *(established wording; for new sellers reframed:
   "what should clients expect from you?")*
7. How should people reach you — WhatsApp, booking, form? *(one-tap confirm from
   region+profession default; never silently guessed)*
8. Site language(s) *(auto from location + their site; one-tap confirm when ambiguous.
   REQUIRED for Kundius delivery: NL/EN)*

Never ask twice. Never replay their answer back for confirmation of a one-liner.
Never ask a photographer "what are you offering?" — irrelevant questions tell the user
we don't understand them.

**Value:** every answer visibly shapes the site. Questions build trust instead of burning it.

**How the questions choose themselves (agreed):** the list of facts we need is fixed and
small — designed by us in advance. For each fact, before asking, we check what we already
worked out from the one line and their work/site/socials:

- Know it confidently → never ask; show it pre-filled, tap to correct.
- Almost sure → confirm with one tap (choices, not typing).
- Don't know → ask, as tappable choices; free text only as last resort.

So the steps VARY per person — someone with a rich portfolio online might see 2 questions,
someone brand new sees 5 — but every possible question is one we designed and tested. The
AI decides **which** questions to show and can personalize their **wording** ("For wedding
shoots, where do you sit on price?") — it never invents a new question or changes the
process. Five is the ceiling, not the count. Open chat is the exception handler (a
"something wrong? tell us" box at the plan step), never the main road.

- **Left rail** = the journey-wide "What we understood" panel (see journey-wide rule) +
  page count / plan limit shown calmly + the build button.
- **One column per page** — whole site in one glance.
- **Real photos in the plan** — their ingested work appears where it will go. The
  difference between "a plan" and "MY site"; nobody else in the market does this.
- **Plain-word section rows** with a one-line description each ("List of your packages
  with prices"). Zero internal vocabulary — no Hero/Testimonial/CTA/Collection, ever.
  Section names here become the user-facing vocabulary EVERYWHERE (editor, reports).
- **Goal badge on every page footer** ("👆 asks visitors to: Message on WhatsApp") —

  carries multiple goals legally: different pages, different actions.
- **Tap powers, deliberately few:** add/remove page (from a designed set), rename,
  reorder, change a page's goal, swap which work leads. NOT section-level rearranging —
  that's the editor's job; offering it here recreates overwhelm.
- Visible hierarchy: photos big, section rows small, goal badge distinct — never a
  wall of same-weight rows.

**Ordering (agreed, from agency test pain):** approving the plan STARTS the build; the
look picker appears DURING the generation wait — waiting time becomes shopping time,
and the reveal lands already dressed in their chosen feel.

**Value:** they correct the plan in seconds instead of discovering problems after the build.

### 5. We write and build it — their facts are law
While they watch, with honest progress (never a fake fast spinner).

Rules we never break:
- Facts they gave are used **exactly** — services, prices, names, words. Never invented,
  never ignored, never "improved."
- Client praise appears word for word.
- Their best work placed deliberately — best first, big.
- Every page ends at its one action.
- Copy talks benefits in the buyer's language, short. No filler paragraphs.

**Value:** what you told us is what appears. This is the trust the whole product stands on.

### 6. The reveal
Their site, their photos, their clients' words, their prices. Not a demo with stock
images — the real thing, first look.

**Value:** this moment is OUR conversion point. It has to land like magic.

### 7. Pick the feel

**Agreed (brainstorm #4): three looks at launch, all from designer files in hand:**

| Look (user-facing feel words) | Source design | Who picks it |
|---|---|---|
| **Calm gallery** — quiet, refined, work breathes | Atelier (Kundius's pick) | premium photographers, architects |
| **Bold editorial** — big type, confident | Kontur | designers, studios, artists |
| **Warm personal** — human, friendly | Pulse | family/wedding photographers, solo freelancers (the 35% warm-demand block) |

- Why three: one = no agency, two = a binary with a loser, three = real choice on one
  screen, no paralysis. Skeleton economics make look #4 a days-job later — scarcity
  now costs nothing.
- **Named in feel words** user-facing; design name is flavor subtitle at most.
- **Free switching forever**, before and after publish — work stays, words stay
  (skeleton guarantees this literally). No fear of wrong choice = faster choice.

**The during-wait design conversation (agreed):** picking the feel is a short
co-design moment while the copy builds — the loved UX. The reveal must feel CHOSEN,
not assigned. Four taps, in order:

| Tap | She sees | What it really is | Options |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | "Which feels like you?" | the look | 3 cards, her photos inside |
| 2 | "How should it breathe?" | spacing/density variant | Spacious / Cozy — two designed states |
| 3 | "Which type suits you?" | curated type pairing within the look | 2 flavors, her headline rendered in each |
| 4 | "Your accent" | palette pick | small row from the look's palette family |

- **Every option is a designed coordinate, never a free knob** — no font dropdown, no
  color wheel. ~48 total combinations, every one designer-drawn and screenshot-tested
  (this IS the skeleton's combo QA surface).
- **Every tap previews on her real content** — her photos, her headline. A swatch grid
  would not have been loved.
- **All four skippable** — defaults are the look's signature settings. An offer, not a
  form. All re-choosable later (free-switching rule), so no tap carries fear.
- Four taps ≈ the generation wait — the wait disappears entirely.
- Deeper styling than these four = editor territory.
- Kundius delivery needs only Atelier; Kontur doubles as the 1-day second-skin spike
  that proves the skeleton economics; Pulse completes the public trio. Nothing blocks
  on three.
- **Bench:** Lumen is public inventory (Kundius rejected it, chose Atelier — no longer
  exclusive) — photography-native, strong look-#4 candidate when demand asks.

**Value:** taste without homework. No decision paralysis, no fear of "wrong choice."

### 8. Fix anything in clicks — never stuck

Editing splits into TWO doors, because her work and her page design are different
activities (agreed):

**8a. The work library — her photos, managed like content, never in the editor.**
Same pattern as the testimonial system (dashboard collection → pages display by
reference → republish): her groups + photos are a first-class thing she owns, and a
gallery on any page SHOWS a group rather than containing photos. The UI is the same
grouping board from onboarding — day 1 and forever, zero new learning. Consequences:

- Add photos → drop into a group → every page showing that group updates. No editor.
- Recategorize → drag photo between group cards → updates site-wide. One move.
- Delete → photos are **hidden, never destroyed** — restorable anytime. We never lose
  her work.
- New photos run the same technical filter + speed pipeline as onboarding (blurry/
  duplicates killed, compressed) — the site stays fast without her knowing why.
- **A category never becomes a page silently.** Category = gallery, always visible
  somewhere (Work page + Prices row if it's a service). Page = explicit one-tap
  promotion, suggested when earned ("Weddings has 32 photos — give it its own page?").
- **New category = new service** → slot machinery wakes: asks its price, generates just
  that row/intro copy, shows before going live. Copy grows incrementally; never a
  site-wide regeneration.
- Skeleton requirement inherited: gallery blocks render **group references**, never
  embedded photo lists.

**The bigger pattern (agreed): this is a CMS, one pattern for all content types.**
Every content type gets the same three parts: (1) a **board** — simple dashboard
management (add/edit/hide/drag), never the page editor; (2) **display by reference** —
page blocks show the collection, edit once updates everywhere; (3) an **own-URL-per-item
switch** — ON for products/case-studies/blog posts (shareable, googleable), OFF for
photos/testimonials (the gallery is the unit, not the photo). Parts 2+3 already exist
in code (`src/modules/collections/`, scale-10: products·services·case-studies·works,
catalog page + `/products/<slug>` item pages — Naayom rides it). What's missing is
part 1, the board — which is why Naayom's product UI feels bad: its only door is the
page editor. The work-library board is the FIRST instance of part 1; built generic,
it later fixes Naayom's products for free. "Collection" stays an internal word forever;
users see "Your work", "Your products", "Your posts".

**8b. The page editor — words, look, layout.** Swap a photo slot, reorder sections,
rewrite any sentence, rearrange a section's layout, change a button. Preview as a
visitor (including phone view) on the same screen — no separate mode.

**Going live (agreed):** workspace shows every change instantly; the PUBLIC site updates
on one tap — "Update site" — with an honest badge until then ("3 changes not yet live")
and a gentle nudge if pending changes sit for days. Why explicit: she controls the
public moment (half-culled uploads never leak), the site stays a static snapshot (speed
promise), and every update is a version she can roll back to.

Editing feels like **correcting**, not building from scratch. The promise is not
"infinitely customizable" — it's "you will never be stuck one click from what you want."

**Value:** the last 10% is theirs. If they can't fix a small detail, they won't publish.
The library door is why the portfolio stays alive after launch.

### 9. Publish
Their own domain or a free one. Loads instantly. Perfect on a phone. Findable on
Google. Done in one sitting — one line to live site.

**Value:** shipped, not almost-shipped.

### 10. Prove it converts

**Agreed (brainstorm #5): three numbers, told as a funnel in human sentences:**

1. "142 people looked at your site this week." *(heartbeat)*
2. "9 of them checked your prices." *(serious-buyer signal — the work is persuading)*
3. "4 reached out — 3 on WhatsApp, 1 by form." *(THE number; everything serves this line)*

Plus two garnishes, never a dashboard: a trend phrase per number ("…twice last week")
and ONE source line ("most visitors came from your Instagram bio link" — the only fact
that changes her behavior).

- **The report comes to her — weekly email (v1; WhatsApp channel later).** Nobody logs
  into dashboards. Weekly rhythm = retention heartbeat; every email re-proves why she pays.
- **Instant lead alerts** stay separate from the weekly rhythm: the moment someone
  reaches out, she knows (existing lead-email pipe, needs multi-tenant).
- **v1 = facts only.** In-report suggestions ("visitors leave on Prices — try a
  different order?") = LATER: they need data volume to be signal, and one bad
  suggestion burns more trust than no suggestion.
- **Spec gap to close:** WhatsApp-tap tracking (outbound click on the action button) —
  without it the most important number undercounts. Views + form sends already tracked.
- **Ready-made promo links (agreed):** we hand her pre-built links for each place she
  promotes — "this one goes in your Instagram bio, this one in your WhatsApp status,
  this one in your email signature." Source tracking by construction, not guesswork —
  and the source line in her report gets exact ("31 came through your Instagram bio
  link"). Also a quiet activation nudge: the links tell her WHERE to promote.
- **v2 bench (keep):** "your most-viewed photo this month" — delight + quietly guides
  her curation.

**Value:** "high-converting" is a claim. Numbers in human words are the proof, and the
reason to stay subscribed.

## The promises (what we never do)

Born from real test pain — each is a testable requirement, not a slogan:

1. Never ask what we can figure out ourselves.
2. Never ask the same thing twice, never replay a one-liner for confirmation.
3. Never ask a question irrelevant to what this person does.
4. Never ignore or override a fact the user gave. Never invent one.
5. Never show internal vocabulary to the user.
6. Never leave the user stuck — every visible thing is correctable in clicks.
7. Never publish something slow or broken on a phone.
8. Never fake progress; never end a step with the user unsure whether it worked.

## The words users see — canonical buyer-words list (brainstorm #7, DRAFT)

One vocabulary, used identically in the site plan, the editor, the reports, and every
email. Rules first:

1. **Second person, about her site:** "Your best work", not "Portfolio section".
2. **Name what it does for the visitor**, never what it's built from. No Hero,
   Testimonial, CTA, Collection, Widget — ever, anywhere user-facing.
3. **Her groups wear her own names** (Weddings, Newborn) — we only name the containers.
4. **Every name carries a one-line description** where space allows — the description
   does the explaining, so names stay short.
5. **One table in code, single source** — plan chips, editor labels, and report
   sentences all read from it. A rename happens once.
6. Profession-adaptive words (shoot / project / case study) come from the same wording
   layer that personalizes questions.

| Internal (never shown) | She sees | One-line description |
|---|---|---|
| hero | **Your promise** | the first thing visitors see |
| featured work | **Your best work** | the pieces that sell you, up front |
| gallery / portfolio | **Your work** (groups wear her names) | everything you want shown, organized |
| testimonials | **What clients say** | real praise, word for word |
| about | **Your story** | who you are and why you |
| services | **What you offer** | the ways people can hire you |
| pricing | **Packages & prices** | what things cost, so buyers pre-qualify |
| process | **How it works** ("how a shoot works" for photographers) | what happens after they reach out |

## Founder in the loop — first customers (agreed, brainstorm #6)

**Principle: observe everything, gate nothing.** No pre-publish approval step — it kills
the one-sitting magic and hides the product's real failure modes behind founder fixes.
The flow keeps its one legal human gate (unserveable lead → manual onboard). Everything
else is watching, not blocking:

- **v1 uses existing admin access, no new infra:** founder views all pages as admin
  today — daily check of new generations/publishes happens there. Event feeds /
  abandon alerts = later, only if the daily check proves insufficient.
- **24-hour quality sweep, framed as gift not gate:** within a day of a publish, founder
  looks at the live site; if something's off, message the user ("founder here — want me
  to tighten your About?"). Early customers retell "the founder personally reviewed my
  site."
- **Every intervention is a QA finding:** each founder touch gets logged (plain notes
  file, one line each) and becomes a prompt fix or backlog item. Interventions are the
  punch list for making intervention unnecessary; the same fix appearing twice = product
  bug, not a service.
- **Human channels:** weekly report + lead alerts send from a reply-able address that
  reaches the founder; early users see an honest "questions? WhatsApp me."
- **Exit:** quality sweep relaxes after 10 consecutive publishes needing zero
  intervention. Abandoned-after-reveal follow-up stays long-term (each one is research).
- **Kundius is outside this framework** — client #1, full concierge, founder drives.
