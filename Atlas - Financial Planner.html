№ 01 Identity

Lex's restraint, with the dial turned toward humanity. Hearth's warmth, with a chart on the page.

Atlas is for buyers whose customers are choosing a person, not a product. The system's job is to make that person feel competent and warm at the same time — sophisticated enough to trust with $2M, human enough to talk to about a kid's tuition or a divorce.

A planner's job is to draw a map. The system's job is to look like the kind of person who'd draw a good one.

The metaphor is cartography — not literal maps, the grammar of maps. Topographic isolines as background texture. Mile markers as section indicators. North arrows as logo finials. Route paths between journey stages. Pin markers on stage cards. The chart is the destination; the topo line is the terrain.

Five commitments

  • Plans, not products. No "products," no "solutions." Language is milestones, paths, stages, the next ten years. Headlines are about life events, not asset classes.
  • One human face. A planner's site needs the planner's face with weight. Big portrait in hero. Big portrait again in About. Same face, twice.
  • Charts as decoration — but real ones. Net-worth-over-time, Monte Carlo cone, Social Security claiming-age comparison. Drawn in SVG, accurate enough to defend.
  • Calm color, single warm accent. Trust hue carries ~10% of viewport; warm accent < 3%. No two-accent compositions.
  • The fee, named. A planner who hides their fee isn't fee-only. Fee schedule sits hero-adjacent, with the dollar number visible.

№ 04 Decoration

Six permitted motifs. All borrowed from cartography. All used sparingly.

  • North arrows. Circle with "N" inside, small triangle at top. Used as logo finial and on the masthead seal.
  • Topographic isolines. Faint concentric ovals as background texture in hero/CTA only. Never as decoration on cards.
  • Pin markers. Outline circle with filled accent dot at center. Used on stage-card tops to mark the journey route.
  • Dashed routes. 16px-pitch dashed line connecting stage cards. Renders the journey as a path.
  • Mile markers. Italic-numeral indices on letters and stage cards. § notation reserved for Lex.
  • Charts that mean something. SVG renderings of real planner artifacts — net-worth projection, Monte Carlo fan, Social Security claiming comparison.

What's banned: stock photos, "AI-powered planning" anything, robo-advisor energy, three-column "Plan/Protect/Grow" feature trios, hidden disclosures, two-accent palettes (Riot territory), display weights above 600.

stage 02The plan

Five stages, one plan.

A financial plan is a long walk in a known direction. We don't sprint. We don't reroute on the news. We map the terrain, set the heading, and move at the pace that gets you there with the least drama.

01· Discover

What is the money for?

Two long conversations. Statements, taxes, deeds, beneficiaries — a clear-eyed picture of where you actually stand.

You walk away withA one-page net-worth statement.
02· Map

Where could it go?

Three scenarios — modeled in Right Capital, stress-tested, and explained in language a tired person can follow.

You walk away withA 10-year projection, three paths.
03· Build

The plan, written.

An 18-page plan: investments, tax, insurance, estate. Not a binder. Each section has a "what this means" paragraph.

You walk away withA plan you'll actually read.
04· Live

The first year.

Implementation, custodian setup, tax-loss harvesting, beneficiary updates. Two check-ins. Email anytime.

You walk away withEverything actually done.
05· Adjust

Years two and on.

Annual reviews, life-event responses, the call when the market drops 18%. The plan changes; the heading doesn't.

You walk away withA planner you keep.

stage 06About me

A practice of one, on purpose.

I left a $14B wirehouse in 2014 because I was tired of selling things people didn't need. I took the CFP exam, sat my first fiduciary client three months later, and have not had a sales call since.

I work with about ten new households a year. I write every plan myself — no associates, no junior planner, no rotating client-service rep. The person who answers your first email is the person who shows up to your last meeting.

I'm a Certified Financial Planner™, a NAPFA member, and a fiduciary by law and by oath. My Form ADV and Form CRS are linked below — read them. They're short and they're plain English.

An honest note

Who I'm not the right planner for.

  • You want active stock-picking, options, or anything labeled "alternative."
  • You want me to manage assets only, without a plan. I don't unbundle.
  • Your household is below ~$500K and you don't have a turning point in the next two years. Vanguard PAS is genuinely better-priced.
  • You want a 24-hour response. My response time is two business days, on purpose.
